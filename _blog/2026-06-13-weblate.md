As an open-source project, AntennaPod doesn't exist in a vacuum. We rely on a wider ecosystem of open-source tools to keep our project running smoothly. And as many of you know, an important piece of our infrastructure is Weblate, the platform where our global community [translates](https://antennapod.org/contribute/translate) AntennaPod into over 40 languages.

When we migrated our app translations to Weblate in 2024, our goal was to bring the localisation of the whole project (app *and* website) under one common open-source roof. But as we settled into our new home, we had to adjust our furniture and habits to make it work. Today, we are proud to share how we used your generous donations to not only to improve Weblate for AntennaPod, but to make it better for everyone.

## Turning donations into upstream development

Thanks to everyone who has been chipping in to our OpenCollective, AntennaPod is in a solid financial position. After [consulting our community](https://forum.antennapod.org/t/proposal-donation-to-weblate/5710) about making a donation to Weblate, in 2025 we approached the Weblate team about it and discussed the biggest friction points we were facing. That got the ball rolling and led to us agreeing to sponsor the work to address those issues.

After meeting with the Weblate team at [FOSDEM 2026](https://antennapod.org/blog/2026/02/fosdem), we realized that some of our requests required deep, structural changes to Weblate's architecture. To make that happen, our community approved a sponsorship of almost €4,000 to fund Weblate's work on these changes.

Following the release of [Weblate 2026.6](https://weblate.org/en/news/archive/20266-different-picture/) earlier this month, all the features we sponsored are available to Weblate users. Our sponsorship money was [transferred](https://opencollective.com/permalink/ex_nvMcEiYh69syF19xoiVIk) to Weblate the next day.

## What we helped improve (and why)

With the goal of making our own lives (and that of our translators) easier, we focussed on a few things.

### Categories

To this day, our website content lives in almost a hundred individual files. To make it easier to manage that content on Weblate we're using a tool to merge them into just four files. This set-up comes with some quirks and extra maintenance, however. We [requested](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/14237) functionality to merge multiple source files into a single one in Weblate, so we could still have just four components but without our odd set-up.

That request was rejected as it would've introduced complexities to Weblate. Together with Weblate's team we concluded we need something else: improvement to categories (collection of components). And that's what we set out to sponsor:
- [Allow applying settings at project and category level and inherit them to components](https://github.com/WeblateOrg/weblate/issues/10897#issuecomment-3831241112) - for example to set a single [contributor agreement at project level](https://github.com/WeblateOrg/weblate/issues/14231)
- [Enable add-ons at category level](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/17858) - to efficiently ensure the same languages are available for all parts of the website, while defining a different set of languages for the Google Play store and the app (annoyingly, Android and the Play store descriptions have different language support and use different language codes)
- [Add a 'Translate' button to category landing pages](https://github.com/WeblateOrg/weblate/issues/11198) - for a one-click process to start translating our entire website (actually the category landing page was switched from Components to Languages, and when clicking on a language there's now a huge 'Translate' button)

### Migration and management

When making the move to Weblate, we were coming from Transifex. It had our history, and that of our contributors'. And it had a way to mark someone a reviewer for a specific language. To ease the migration, sponsored these changes:
- [Expose ‘add comment’ API endpoint](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/14232) - with the idea that we would migrate source string and translation comments (in the end we didn't migrate comments because by the time we wrapped up the sponsorship process we figured many of the comments would've been outdated anyway, and we didn't want to spend the time on a script that to extract comments from Transifex and push them to Weblate)
- [Add separate ‘post announcements’ permission](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/8974) - to allow our new translations coordinator to inform all our translators of upcoming releases without the need to give them admin permissions (with associated risks of accidentally messing up configuration)
- [Allow setting language permission at user level](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/14233) - to allow users to be marked as reviewer for a specific language and prevent them from (accidentally) locking other languages

## A win-win for the ecosystem

For AntennaPod's localisation, these improvements mean a significantly reduced attack surface and a streamlined workflow. But the best part of this? Every other project using Weblate gets these features for free.
This is exactly what community-funded open source is supposed to look like: users supporting an app, and in turn that app contributing to the platforms it relies on.

Thank you to everyone who contributed, donated or helped AntennaPod in any other way. You made this happen!
