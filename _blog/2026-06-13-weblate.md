As an open source project, AntennaPod doesn't exist in a vacuum. We rely on a wide ecosystem of tools to keep our project running smoothly. One of those tools is Weblate, the platform where our global community [translates](https://antennapod.org/contribute/translate) AntennaPod into over 40 languages.

In 2022 we [started](/blog/2022/01/website-translations) translating our website through Weblate. In 2024 we migrated our app translations, to unify the localization of our entire project on one open-source platform. As we adapted to this new system, we identified ways to improve it. Today, we're proud to share how your generous donations helped us enhance Weblate—not just for AntennaPod, but for everyone.

## Turning donations into upstream development

Thanks to everyone who has contributed to our OpenCollective, AntennaPod is in a solid financial position. After [consulting our community](https://forum.antennapod.org/t/proposal-donation-to-weblate/5710) about a donation to Weblate, we approached the Weblate team in 2025 to discuss our biggest friction points.

When we met the Weblate team at [FOSDEM 2026](https://antennapod.org/blog/2026/02/fosdem), we realized some requests required major architectural changes. To fund this work, our community approved a sponsorship of almost €4,000.

With the release of [Weblate 2026.6](https://weblate.org/en/news/archive/20266-different-picture/) earlier this month, all the features we sponsored are available to Weblate users. The funds were [transferred](https://opencollective.com/permalink/ex_nvMcEiYh69syF19xoiVIk) the next day.

## What we helped improve (and why)

To make life easier for our team and translators, we focused on a few key improvements.

### Categories

Our website content is spread across almost 100 individual files. We use a tool to merge them into just four files to keep things manageable in Weblate. This setup, however, has quirks and requires extra maintenance.

We initially [requested](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/14237) a feature to merge multiple source files into one in Weblate, so we could keep four components but drop our custom setup. Weblate rejected this request because it would add complexity.

Together with their team, we decided on a different approach: improving categories (groups of components). So that's what we sponsored:
- [Allow settings to be applied at project or category level](https://github.com/WeblateOrg/weblate/issues/10897#issuecomment-3831241112) and inheriting them in components, for example to [set a single contributor agreement](https://github.com/WeblateOrg/weblate/issues/14231) for the whole project
- [Enable add-ons at category level](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/17858), for example to ensure all website parts are translated into the same set of languages while allowing different languages for the app and Google Play store texts
- [Making it easier to start translating from category pages](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/11198) by making the language list the category landing page and displaying a clear 'Translate' button on every language page

### Migration and management

We migrated to Weblate from Transifex, which contained our history and contributor data, and offered specific permission options. To adapt to our new setup we sponsored these changes:
- [Allow adding comments via the API](https://github.com/weblateOrg/weblate/issues/14232) to potentially migrate comments from Transifex (though in the end we didn’t as we suspected the comments were outdated)
- [Add a separate permission for posting announcements](https://github.com/WeblateOrg/weblate/issues/8974) to allow our translation coordinator to inform translators of upcoming releases without full admin rights
- [Allow setting language permissions at user level](https://github.com/WeblateOrg/weblate/issues/14233) to make users reviewer for a specific language, preventing accidental changes to other languages

## A win-win for the ecosystem

For AntennaPod, these improvements mean better security and a more streamlined workflow. But the best part? Other open source projects using Weblate get to benefit as well, for free.
This is what community-funded open source should look like: users support an app, and in turn, that app improves the whole open source ecosystem.

Thank you to everyone who contributed, donated or helped AntennaPod in any other way. You made this happen!
